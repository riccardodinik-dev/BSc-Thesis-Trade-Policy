# The Macroeconomic Consequences of the 47th U.S. President's Trade Policy on the EU Member States

Bachelor's Dissertation

---

## Abstract

This dissertation investigates the macroeconomic consequences of U.S. protectionist trade policies on the economic growth of European Union Member States.

The study combines international trade theory with empirical macroeconomic analysis to evaluate the potential effects of tariff escalation under different policy scenarios. After reviewing the historical evolution of trade policy—from Mercantilism and Ricardo's Comparative Advantage to the modern WTO framework—the dissertation develops a counterfactual empirical framework to estimate how U.S. tariff shocks could affect EU GDP growth.

The empirical analysis focuses on two transmission mechanisms:

- Export channel
- Import (retaliation) channel

The estimated models are then employed to simulate alternative tariff scenarios and quantify their expected macroeconomic effects across EU Member States.

## Methodology

The empirical section combines:

- Panel data econometrics
- Fixed-effects regression models
- Counterfactual policy simulations
- Export and import channel decomposition
- Robustness and diagnostic testing
- Scenario analysis

The dissertation evaluates the potential macroeconomic impact of alternative U.S. tariff policies under multiple simulated trade environments.

---

## Main Contributions

- Reviews the historical evolution of international trade theory and institutions.
- Explains the welfare effects of tariffs under both small- and large-country frameworks.
- Estimates the relationship between trade exposure and GDP growth for EU Member States.
- Simulates alternative tariff scenarios using counterfactual econometric models.
- Discusses policy implications for European trade strategy and possible retaliatory measures.
